
Late fusion is when we are combining after both modalities have produced their final probability score. Here, we take the imaging model, and the tabular model to aim to get a better prediction than either model alone.

The Imaging model is essentially a ResNet that looked at MRI scans, and has its predictions saved in a CSV 
The tabular model is a sklearn model trained on clinical features, saved as a pkl file.

The script loads both, runs them on the same 1718 test patients and then attempts late fusion in three ways:
1. Simple Average (50/50)
    - For each patient, add imaging score and tabular score together and then divide by 2
    - Utility : Averaging could potentially smooth out individual errors made by imaging and tabular data 
    - Why not optimal: Assumes that imaging and tabular are equally good. 

2. Weighted Average (Learn the best ratio of combination)
    - We try every possible weight combination from 0 to 1 in steps of 0.05. 
    - The relationship that works best is 0.35 imaging, 0.65 tabular. This might be because tabular alone outperformed imaging alone (0.872 vs 0.833) 
    - Why not optimal: Assumes that the relationship is always linear 

3. Logistic Regression (Learn optimal combination)
    - Instead of manually finding the best combination, we let the model learn it. Here we did it by using cross-validation, and splitting 1718 patients into 5 groups, trained the combination formula on 4 groups, tested it on the 5th group, and repeated it 5 times so there is no bias in the way the test group is selected.
    - Essentially works on seeing how much to trust imaging, how much to trust tabular and a bias adjustment
    - It is more optimal than weighted average because unlike it, it never predicts on patients its trained on. That is also why its AUC is slightly lower
    - Why not optimal: Assumes that the relationship is always linear, and still applies the same weights to every patient regardless of their clinical profile.

Optimal would be to have a non-linear model which adapts , such as a neural networ that takes both scores as inputs and learns complex non-linear relationships between them. However, doing so on this many patients runs the ris of overfitting too much, so logistic regression stands as the best choice given our data size.
